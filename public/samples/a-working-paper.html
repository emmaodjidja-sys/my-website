This page is an account of what I work on, why, and with whom. I am an evaluator and applied researcher. I design and implement monitoring and evaluation systems for programmes operating under the constraints typical of fragile and conflict-affected settings — security incidents, attrition, absent baseline data, partner turnover. My published work, listed at §3, spans health systems, nutrition, infectious disease, and the climate–conflict–food insecurity nexus across West Africa, East Africa, the Sahel, North Africa, and South Asia.

§ 1 — Background

The through-line

If a programme works, it should be proven using sound evidence, not anecdotes.1 That conviction has shaped twelve years of work across South Sudan, Burundi, Ghana, the Sahel, Tunisia, and Sri Lanka — testing methods where they are likeliest to fail. I read epidemiology at Queen Margaret University in Edinburgh. I currently lead monitoring, evaluation, research, and learning at GCERF, and edit the case-based evaluations section of the Journal of MultiDisciplinary Evaluation.

Most of what I have learned about evaluation, I have learned by watching well-specified designs collapse on contact with the field, and by then asking what salvageable evidence remains.

Note 1 From the field-notes (Bujumbura, 2020-04-12). First articulated in writing as a section heading for an unpublished internal review of M&E systems at Village Health Works.
§ 2 — Practice

What I do

I design evaluations around a stubborn principle: when conditions on the ground refuse to cooperate with the plan, the design has to be flexible enough to produce evidence anyway. In practice that means a working preference for matched difference-in-differences, propensity-score matching, event-study designs, and mixed methods2 — chosen for what the setting can support, not for what looks rigorous on paper.

Working specification for the matched DiD applied to Ghana's CHPS health-financing reform (Odjidja et al., 2022):
Yidt = α + β·(Postt × Treati) + θd + γt + εidt
with propensity-score weights on district-level pre-period covariates and cluster-robust SEs at the district.

Twenty-nine peer-reviewed articles to date. I work in English and French, and have lived field experience in nine countries.

Note 2 Mixed methods, here, mostly mean integrating qualitative key-informant rounds with the quantitative panel — not a separate strand. The qualitative is for understanding mechanism; the quantitative is for bounding the effect.
