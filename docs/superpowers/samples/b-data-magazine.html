Twelve years of evidence, told as data.

A monitoring & evaluation specialist's accounting of the programmes, places, methods and findings that have occupied his career — and the unfashionable lesson the field keeps confirming.

By Emmanuel Nene Odjidja, in Geneva · Updated 16 May 2026

If a programme works, it should be proven using sound evidence, not anecdotes. That has been the through-line of twelve years of work — across South Sudan, Burundi, Ghana, the Sahel, Tunisia, and Sri Lanka — testing methods where they are likeliest to fail. The chart above is a partial record: twenty-nine articles, eight years, five themes.

Most of what I have learned about evaluation I have learned by watching well-specified designs collapse on contact with the field, and by then asking what salvageable evidence remains. In displacement camps without baseline data, in health systems where every security incident resets the timeline, in nutrition surveys across three countries with no consistent sampling frame — the textbook design is rarely available. The work, then, is in the choices that follow.

The most valuable evaluations are not the most methodologically sophisticated. They are the ones designed with enough pragmatism to survive first contact with the field. — On evidence in fragile settings, 2025

The geography matters because the method is not separable from the setting. Difference-in-differences in Ghana's CHPS health-financing reform looked like one thing; the same design transplanted to nutrition surveillance in active-conflict South Sudan would have produced nothing worth defending.¹

By the method

A working preference, in order of frequency: matched difference-in-differences, propensity-score matching, event-study designs, and mixed methods. The qualitative strand is rarely the headline — but it is what tells you why the quantitative finding holds, where it won't generalise, and what would break it.
